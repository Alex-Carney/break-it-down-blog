# The AI Primer

**Technology, adoption, and enterprise value**

*Break it Down | February 2026*

The gap between what AI can demonstrably do and what most organisations have managed to get from it is the most expensive unanswered question in enterprise technology. This primer examines where it comes from, what sustains it, and what the organisations that have closed it did differently.

---

## Key findings

**AI works at the task level.** Six controlled studies, spanning more than 11,000 workers, show productivity gains of 15 to 56 per cent — on specific tasks. Outside the technology's capability boundary, AI makes skilled professionals measurably worse.

**Most implementations fail to deliver enterprise value.** Across McKinsey, BCG/MIT Sloan, and S&P Global, 70 to 80 per cent of organisations report no significant business value from AI.

**The failures are primarily organisational, not technological.** Four of the top five root causes of AI project failure are organisational: misunderstood problems, inadequate data, technology-first mentality, and insufficient infrastructure planning.

**The highest-leverage investments are in foundations, not models.** The AI model changes every three to six months. Data infrastructure, integration architecture, and organisational capability retain their value across model generations.

## 1. The gap

When American manufacturers first adopted electric power in the 1890s, the standard approach was to bolt electric motors onto factory floors designed around a central steam-driven shaft. The layout, the workflow, the organisational structure — all stayed the same. Productivity gains were negligible for roughly twenty years. The shortfall was attributed to the technology, to the workforce, to insufficient investment. The actual constraint was the factory itself.

The parallel to enterprise AI is not metaphorical. It is structural.

Nearly nine out of ten organisations now use artificial intelligence. Fewer than one in ten report meaningful financial returns from it. The scale of the commitment is not in question: in 2025, the five largest American technology companies will spend between $364 billion and $400 billion on AI infrastructure, roughly four times what they spent three years ago. Capital markets have generally rewarded the commitment.

Before examining the evidence, it is worth tracing the numbers most organisations currently rely on.

<Callout type="myth" title="The numbers everyone cites">

Like the factories of the 1890s, the organisations bolting AI onto existing structures are getting negligible returns. The evidence assembled in this primer points to the same structural dynamic: capable tools meeting operating environments built for a different way of working. The organisations that have redesigned their environments — a minority, roughly 6 per cent — are the ones closing the gap.

## 2. How the technology works

A useful heuristic: if you cannot explain to a non-technical colleague what your AI system is *actually doing* — not what the vendor says it does, but what computation is occurring — you cannot evaluate its risks, its costs, or its limitations. Most purchasing decisions are made without this understanding, and it shows in the outcomes.

AI is not one technology. It is a nested set of increasingly specific technologies, each with different mechanics, costs, and limitations.

The critical inversion is in the spend-vs-value columns. What most people mean when they say "AI" in 2026 is generative AI — chatbots and content generation. What most enterprises actually deploy in production is traditional machine learning: predictive models, classification, recommendation engines. The layer that generates the most excitement is also the layer with the least documented enterprise value per dollar spent.

That distinction matters for the rest of this primer. Understanding what the technology actually does — and does not do — at each layer determines whether an investment thesis holds up.

### The core mechanism

An LLM is, at bottom, a next-token prediction engine. Given a sequence of text, it predicts the most statistically probable next word. It processes the input through billions of parameters encoding patterns from training data. The output is a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary. It samples, appends, and repeats.

That is the entire operation. Everything a language model produces — fluent prose, working code, apparent reasoning — emerges from next-token prediction applied at enormous scale. There is no separate understanding module. There is no database being queried. There is a statistical model generating the most plausible continuation, millions of times in sequence.

<Callout type="warning" title="Plausibility ≠ truth">
    LLMs optimise for what text *would plausibly come next*, not for whether that text is factually correct. When a model generates a confident citation to a paper that does not exist, it is producing text that *looks like* what a real citation looks like. OpenAI's more powerful o3 reasoning models show **33–51% hallucination rates** on factual benchmarks — *more than double* the rates of the less powerful o1 models. More capability does not automatically mean more reliability.
</Callout>

Output quality depends heavily on input quality. The same model produces dramatically different results depending on how the prompt is structured. And a model's knowledge is frozen at training time — it has no access to current events or internal data unless that access is provided explicitly.

## 3. What AI can actually do

If you are evaluating an AI investment, the starting question is not what the technology could do in theory. It is what the controlled evidence shows it can do in practice, in conditions that resemble an actual organisation.

The evidence base is stronger than most people realise — and more bounded than most vendor pitches suggest.

### The controlled studies

Six controlled or quasi-controlled studies form the core of current knowledge about AI's real-world productivity impact.

### The consistent finding

Across all studies, one result appears every time.

<Callout type="evidence" title="AI compresses the skill distribution">
    AI disproportionately helps less experienced workers. Brynjolfsson's least experienced agents saw roughly double the improvement of the most experienced. Noy and Zhang's lower-ability writers saw the largest gains. An OECD review (July 2025) confirmed the pattern across settings. AI is an equaliser, not an amplifier.
</Callout>

<Callout type="insight" title="The Solow paradox, 2026 edition">
    Economist Robert Solow observed in 1987 that "you can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics." Task-level AI gains have not yet materialised in aggregate economic data. The gap between "AI helps individuals on specific tasks" and "AI makes organisations measurably more profitable" is real, and the evidence in this primer suggests the explanation is the same one that applied to electrification: the gains cannot materialise until the operating environment is redesigned to accommodate them.
</Callout>

### The jagged frontier

Dell'Acqua's study with 758 BCG consultants documented something prior research had not shown so precisely. Consultants received two types of tasks: some within GPT-4's capability boundary, some just outside it.

On tasks where AI was not competent, having access to it made skilled professionals worse than having no AI at all.

Dell'Acqua and his co-authors called this the "jagged technological frontier." The boundary between what AI can and cannot do is not a clean line. It is irregular, unintuitive, and difficult to predict in advance. A model might produce flawless legal research on contract interpretation and fabricate case law entirely in an adjacent domain.

The finding has been independently corroborated. Joshua Gans published a formal economic model through the National Bureau of Economic Research (January 2026) demonstrating that scaling improves average quality without eliminating jaggedness. Zhou et al. (*Nature*, 2024) confirmed that larger models improve in most areas but become *less* reliable in others. Better models are still jagged.

### When AI makes things worse

It is not simply that AI fails to help outside its frontier. It actively degrades performance — through a human mechanism, not a technical one.

Dell'Acqua's earlier research found that HR recruiters given access to high-quality AI (85% accuracy) performed *worse* than those given low-quality AI (75% accuracy). Higher apparent quality produced less scrutiny and more blind trust.

<StatCallout
    value="80% → 20%"
    label="Accuracy drop among less experienced radiologists when AI predictions were incorrect. They followed the AI's wrong answer instead of their own training."
    source="Systematic review, AI & Society (2025), 35 studies"

/>

A study in *The Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology* (2025) documented an even more concerning pattern: after AI was introduced for colonoscopy assistance, adenoma detection rates in non-AI-assisted procedures dropped from 28.4 to 22.4 per cent. Clinicians who had been using AI became less attentive when performing procedures without it. The technology was creating a dependency that degraded baseline human capability.

<Callout type="evidence" title="The benchmark gap">
    SWE-bench evaluates AI on curated coding tasks: top scores of **80.9%**. SWE-Lancer uses real freelance tasks with monetary stakes: even the best models succeed only **26.2%** of the time. Same models. Different conditions. The gap measures the distance between a controlled evaluation and actual work.
</Callout>

## 5. Why implementations fail

The pattern that emerges from the evidence is counterintuitive. An organisation can have the right model, the right data, the right budget, and the right talent — and still fail. The RAND Corporation's 2024 peer-reviewed study found that four of the five top root causes are organisational, not technical. McKinsey's 2025 survey reinforces the point: of the 88 per cent of organisations using AI, roughly 1 per cent report being at a mature, scaled stage.

The four cases that follow are not cautionary tales selected for shock value. They are the normal distribution of outcomes — and each illuminates a different structural dynamic that the evidence base predicts.

### The readiness illusion: IBM Watson for Oncology

IBM checked every readiness box. CEO-level sponsorship. More than $4 billion in total investment. Partnerships with Memorial Sloan Kettering and MD Anderson — two of the world's most prestigious oncology institutions. More than 2,000 dedicated employees.

<FigureEmbed
    number={7}
    title="The Readiness Scorecard"
    description="IBM Watson for Oncology scored green on every conventional readiness dimension — executive sponsorship, budget, talent, data, technology, market position. The outcome was red. Explore each dimension to see how strengths interacted to produce failure."
>
    <WatsonScorecard client:load />

The failures emerged not from any single dimension but from interactions between them.

Watson's NLP, built for Jeopardy!-style factual retrieval, could not parse the ambiguity of medical text. When real clinical data proved too inconsistent for training, clinicians began creating synthetic cases — hypothetical patients designed to be clean and complete. Watson learned what doctors *thought should happen* rather than what *actually happened*. Training was performed by one or two physicians whose preferences became embedded in the system.

IBM's aggressive marketing created pressure to deploy before the system was ready. Internal documents revealed that Watson had generated unsafe recommendations, including suggesting drugs with severe contraindications. The University of Texas audited the MD Anderson partnership and found $62 million spent before the project was cancelled.

<CaseConclusion>
    Readiness is not additive. World-class talent combined with synthetic data produced biased training. A massive budget combined with an unrealistic timeline produced premature deployment. Strong individual dimensions interacted to produce weak outcomes.
</CaseConclusion>

### The integration blindspot: NHS England

In 2023, NHS England launched a £21 million programme to deploy AI for chest diagnostics across 66 hospital trusts. The AI tools were commercially available, regulator-approved, and clinically validated.

The AI worked. The integration did not.

NHS trusts operate variable, ageing infrastructure. No two trusts had identical systems. Procurement ran four to ten months longer than planned. Each trust required separate governance approvals. Even when technically installed, the tools needed to fit into actual workflows: where do AI flags appear? Who reviews them? What happens when the AI and the radiologist disagree?

By June 2025, 23 of 66 trusts — more than a third — were still not using the tools in clinical practice. Where AI was deployed, radiologists reported higher levels of burnout due to added workflow complexity.

<CaseConclusion>
    The model is 10 to 20 per cent of the system. The integration layer between a working model and institutional reality is where most implementations break — and the layer most proposals omit.
</CaseConclusion>

### The scale distortion: Zillow Offers

Zillow's Zestimate was, by any reasonable measure, a successful AI product. By 2021, it estimated home values within 2 per cent of selling price for on-market homes.

In 2018, Zillow launched Zillow Offers: using the same algorithmic foundations to make cash offers, renovate, and resell. The leap was from *estimating* values — an informational function where errors are low-cost — to *transacting* on values, where errors mean owning depreciating assets.

<FigureEmbed
    number={8}
    title="The Scale Transition"
    description="The Zestimate and Zillow Offers used the same algorithmic foundations. The shift from informational to transactional introduced categorically different failure modes."
>

The problems at transactional scale were not amplified versions of problems from the informational product. They were qualitatively different. Overpaying was catastrophic. Sellers whose homes were overvalued disproportionately accepted offers, creating adversarial selection the informational product had never surfaced.

Under competitive pressure, Zillow launched "Project Ketchup": using the Zestimate directly as cash offers, preventing pricing experts from modifying algorithmic valuations, raising bids to win deals. In November 2021, CEO Rich Barton announced the shutdown.

    value="$48B → $16B"
    label="Zillow's market capitalisation drop in nine months. Losses exceeded $500 million. Competitors using similar models weathered the same conditions. Their algorithms adjusted. Zillow's did not."

    accent="coral"
/>

<CaseConclusion>
    POC success is an insufficient predictor of production success. Scale introduces categorically new failure modes that cannot be observed at pilot scale.
</CaseConclusion>

### The organisational immune response

The fourth pattern is the hardest to document because it is the most diffuse. There is no $500 million write-down. There is a working AI system that nobody uses.

BCG's Deborah Lovich describes the dynamic as "organ rejection": AI technically deployed but culturally refused. Leaders who frame AI purely as a productivity tool are essentially telling employees not to engage with it, because employees hear "productivity tool" as "tool that replaces what I do."

<StatCallout

A Brisbane hospital study (*JMIR*, 2026) traced the pattern in clinical practice. Before implementation, radiologists expressed cautious willingness. ## 6. Where it has worked — and what it cost

The failure patterns above are the majority experience. But they are not the only experience. Roughly 6 per cent of organisations have closed the adoption-value gap. Understanding what they did differently — and what it actually cost them — matters more than cataloguing what went wrong.

### Klarna: The full arc

In early 2024, Swedish fintech Klarna launched an OpenAI-powered customer service assistant. The first-month results were extraordinary: 2.3 million conversations handled, two-thirds of all customer service interactions. Resolution time dropped from 11 minutes to under 2 minutes. Repeat contacts fell 25 per cent. Klarna projected a $40 million profit improvement for 2024 and publicly described the system as doing the work of 700 full-time employees.

The numbers were real. Klarna had, by any standard metric, achieved one of the most successful enterprise AI deployments on record.

What followed is the most instructive case in this primer — not because it failed, but because it succeeded, overcorrected, failed partially, and then succeeded again differently. The arc is worth following in full.

>

By mid-2025, CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski acknowledged what customers had been experiencing: "Cost was a predominant evaluation factor" in organising support, and the result was "lower quality." Klarna had overpivoted — optimising for cost containment without adequately investing in the cases where human judgment was irreplaceable. Complex disputes, fraud investigations, and emotionally charged interactions were being routed to AI systems that could not handle them well. Customer satisfaction on simple queries remained strong, but the long tail of difficult cases eroded trust.

Klarna's correction was instructive. Rather than retreating from AI, the company recalibrated: rehiring human agents for complex interactions, building a hybrid model where AI handles routine high-volume queries and humans handle everything requiring judgment, empathy, or nuanced decision-making. By Q3 2025, the AI assistant was handling the equivalent of 853 full-time employees' workload — *more* than the original 700 — while human agents handled escalations that AI could not.

## 7. The system underneath

The four failure patterns from Section 5 are not independent. They are aspects of the same phenomenon: AI components interacting with organisational systems in ways that neither component-level evaluation nor conventional project management anticipates.

### Four dynamics, one system

**The readiness paradox.** Organisations that score well on individual readiness dimensions can still fail. Cisco's survey (N=7,985): 88 per cent adoption, only 13 per cent fully ready. MIT CISR's maturity model (N=721): the 7 per cent at the most advanced stage outperformed their industry by 17.1 percentage points on growth and 10.4 on profit; the 28 per cent at the earliest stage underperformed by 12.6 on growth and 9.6 on profit.

    value="30pp"
    label="Performance spread between the most and least mature AI organisations — 17.1pp outperformance at the top versus 12.6pp underperformance at the bottom."
    source="MIT CISR Enterprise AI Maturity Model (N=721)"

**The integration gap.** The translation layer between AI capability and business process is where most implementations break. Integration adds 30–50 per cent beyond initial estimates. Gartner documents 500–1,000 per cent cost estimation errors at the pilot-to-production step. Capital One/Forrester (N=500): 73 per cent cite data quality as the primary barrier.

**The scale distortion.** Scale introduces failure modes invisible at pilot. S&P Global: the average organisation scrapped 46 per cent of POCs before production. Deloitte: realistic ROI timelines are 2–4 years against typical executive expectations of 7–12 months. The mismatch may itself drive premature cancellation — organisations declaring failure on initiatives that simply have not had time to mature.

**The organisational immune response.** BCG's 10-20-70 finding quantifies the mechanism: 70 per cent of value comes from business process transformation. RAND: four of five failure root causes are organisational. McKinsey: leadership indecision is a stronger predictor of failure than technology limitations. Improving the technology does not solve the organisational problem.

### How they cascade

Poor data quality reduces model accuracy → reduces user trust → reduces adoption → prevents ROI demonstration → causes sponsorship to withdraw → kills the project.

A compressed timeline forces teams to skip integration testing → surfaces as production errors → erodes confidence.

An underfunded change management programme produces a technically sound system that nobody uses → generating no data to improve with.

Addressing one dynamic without addressing the others moves the failure point downstream. It does not eliminate it.

## Closing

The organisations that electrified successfully in the early twentieth century did not do so by buying better motors. They did so by redesigning the factory: the physical layout, the workflow, the organisational structure, the skills of the workforce. The motor mattered. It was necessary. But it was the cheapest and most replaceable component in the system.

The evidence in this primer, drawn from controlled studies spanning more than 11,000 workers, case analyses of billion-dollar failures, and surveys covering thousands of organisations, points to the same structural logic.

AI works. It works reliably and measurably at the task level. The organisations that have captured value from it — roughly 6 per cent — did so not by finding a better model but by building the system around it: the data infrastructure, the integration layer, the change management investment, the realistic timelines. The 94 per cent that have not captured value are, for the most part, bolting motors onto steam-powered floors.

The motors will keep getting better. They will get better on a timeline measured in months. The question that determines whether that improvement converts to value is not which motor to buy. It is whether the factory is ready for it.
