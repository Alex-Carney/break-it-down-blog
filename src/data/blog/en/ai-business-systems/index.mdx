# The Hidden Variable in AI Adoption

**In a controlled study, 52 developers used the same AI tool on the same task. Outcomes ranged from 24% to 86%. Sixteen studies trace what drives that 62-point gap — and why it's invisible from the outside.**

*Break it Down. | February 2026*

---

## 1. The Gap

Fifty-two developers sat down to learn a new coding library. All used the same AI assistant. All had the same amount of time.

When researchers scored them afterward, the best-performing group averaged 86%. The worst averaged 24%.

A 62-percentage-point spread, among developers using identical tools on an identical task. The gap between the highest-performing and lowest-performing groups of AI users was larger than the gap between most AI users and people who used no AI at all.

Something was driving that variation. It was not the tool, nor skill level, nor task difficulty. And from the outside, watching these developers work, the difference was nearly imperceptible. [1]

This article examines what that variable is, where it appears across sixteen studies, and why it warrants more attention than whatever AI model an organization has most recently deployed.

---

## 2. Productivity Gains and Their Boundary Conditions

Multiple well-designed studies have documented meaningful productivity improvements from AI tools. BCG consultants using GPT-4 completed 12% more tasks, 25% faster, at 40% higher quality. [2] Customer service agents at a Fortune 500 company resolved 14% more issues per hour, with the largest gains among newer workers. [4] GitHub Copilot users finished coding tasks 56% faster. [11] A meta-analysis covering thousands of participants places the median productivity improvement between 12% and 25%.

These findings are credible and consistent. They are also incomplete.

The same BCG study that produced the 40% quality gain included a second set of tasks requiring integration of information across sources, evaluation of conflicting evidence, and judgment rather than execution of established patterns. On those tasks, consultants who used AI performed 19 percentage points worse than the control group that used no AI at all. Same participants, same tool, same session, opposite outcome. [2]

The METR study reinforces this pattern. Sixteen developers, working on their own projects, were 19% slower with AI assistance. They did not notice. Before the study, they predicted AI would save them 24% of their time. Afterward, they believed it had saved them approximately 20%. The measured result was a 19% increase in time to completion. [3]

The distance between perceived and actual performance was 43 percentage points. Participants *perceived* significant time savings while incurring a significant time cost.

<Figure3PerceptionGap client:load />

Taken together, the evidence does not support the conclusion that AI uniformly improves or uniformly degrades performance. The same tool, used by the same people, in some cases during the same session, produces dramatically different outcomes depending on a variable that is not immediately visible.

## 3. Six Patterns, One Study, 62 Points

The Anthropic study is distinctive because it moved beyond outcome measurement to process observation. Shen and Tamkin recorded screen sessions, documenting what the fifty-two developers did with the AI turn by turn, prompt by prompt. They identified six distinct behavioral patterns. [1]

Three patterns produced high scores (65–86%). Three produced low scores (24–39%). The patterns were unrelated to how frequently developers used AI; some high scorers used it heavily, and some low scorers barely engaged with it. The differentiator was structural. It was *how* they engaged with the tool.

The highest-scoring pattern, Generation-Then-Comprehension (averaging 86%), followed a consistent sequence: the developer asked the AI to generate code, then spent several follow-up turns probing the output. "Why did you structure this that way?" "What happens if the input is unexpected?" "Walk me through how this handles errors." The AI served as a generator; the developer then interrogated the output until they understood it.

The lowest-scoring pattern, AI Delegation (averaging 39%), was nearly identical in its external appearance: the developer asked the AI to generate code, received it, and moved on. No follow-up questions. No interrogation. No comprehension check.

The time difference between these two approaches was approximately four minutes.

The two workflows merit direct comparison. Both developers open a chat window. Both type a request. Both receive code. The only visible difference is two follow-up questions taking roughly four minutes. Everything else is identical.

From a manager's dashboard, a time-tracking tool, or any conventional productivity metric, these two developers are nearly indistinguishable. The faster developer may even appear more productive by standard measures.

The outcome gap between them is 47 percentage points.

<Figure1ThesisToggle client:load />

This finding reframes the productivity question. The relevant variable is not whether AI is used, how often, or which model. It is what happens in the minutes after the AI produces its output.

## 4. What Follow-Up Questions Actually Do

Those follow-up questions do more than check the AI's work. When you ask why a solution is structured a particular way, you are forced to engage with the underlying logic — the same mental work that debugging an error would have forced naturally. The high-scoring developers were recreating that productive friction on purpose: using AI to generate the case and then thinking through it themselves.

Developers who delegated to AI without follow-up encountered a median of 1 error during their session. Developers who used no AI encountered a median of 3 errors. [1] This initially appears to favor AI assistance: fewer errors, less friction. But errors are the mechanism through which practitioners build mental models of a system. Each error forces engagement with *why* something fails, which builds comprehension of how the system actually operates.

The developers who asked follow-up questions were deliberately reconstructing this productive friction. Asking "why does this approach work?" or "what would happen if the input changed?" forces the same mental engagement as debugging an error — but faster and more targeted. These developers used AI to accelerate learning rather than substitute for it.

The developers who delegated used AI to bypass learning entirely. They produced working code without understanding why it works. On the assessment measuring actual comprehension, they scored 17% lower than the group that used no AI. [1] AI did not merely fail to support their learning; it actively interfered with it by removing the friction that forces understanding.

This maps directly onto the BCG results. Tasks where AI improved outcomes (+40% quality) were structured tasks where the output is the deliverable: pattern matching, established procedures, known solutions. Delegation is effective because the AI's answer is, in fact, the answer. Tasks where AI degraded outcomes (−19%) were judgment tasks where the output depends on understanding the problem space: integrating conflicting evidence, evaluating contextual fit, weighing tradeoffs that depend on information the AI does not have. On these tasks, delegation produces confident answers to the wrong question, and the practitioner lacks the understanding to recognize the error. [2]

The BCG researchers described this as the "jagged frontier" — the irregular and difficult-to-predict boundary between tasks where AI excels and tasks where it fails. The critical finding was not about the frontier itself. It was that practitioners who crossed it without recognizing they had done so — applying delegation to judgment tasks — performed worse than those who used no AI at all.

The variable driving the 62-point spread becomes legible at this point. It is not AI fluency in the conventional sense of prompt engineering or tool proficiency. It is the capacity to decompose work into its components, to distinguish which parts are pattern execution (where delegation is effective) from which parts are judgment calls (where the AI must be interrogated rather than trusted), and to maintain that distinction consistently even when delegation is faster and requires less effort.

## 5. The Dependency Trap

If the variable were purely a matter of technique, a training program would be sufficient. Two additional findings make the problem structurally more difficult.

The first is cognitive. The Shen study measured knowledge *after* the session. Developers who delegated did not merely produce lower-quality work; they built less understanding of the system they were working with. [1] This matters because evaluating AI output requires domain understanding. If delegation erodes understanding, and understanding is the prerequisite for recognizing when delegation is failing, the result is a reinforcing loop: delegation reduces the capacity to identify when delegation is inappropriate.

This dynamic has been observed clinically. Budzyń and colleagues studied endoscopists who used an AI detection system for three months, then had it removed. Their detection accuracy dropped 22% relative to their pre-AI baseline — not relative to AI-assisted performance, but relative to where they started before any AI involvement. Three months of AI assistance made the physicians measurably worse at the core clinical task the AI was designed to support. [6]

<Figure4TwoLoops client:load />

The second finding is motivational. Wu and colleagues conducted four experiments measuring how AI collaboration affects willingness to work independently. After collaborating with AI, participants reported decreased intrinsic motivation for unassisted work and increased boredom, while simultaneously reporting greater perceived control. They recognized their capacity to do the work independently. They preferred not to. [7]

This creates a second reinforcing loop: AI collaboration reduces the motivation to practice independently, which reduces solo practice, which reduces independent capability, which increases AI reliance. The cognitive loop erodes the *ability* to evaluate AI output. The motivational loop erodes the *desire* to do so. Together, they form a dependency in which both the capacity and the inclination for critical evaluation degrade simultaneously, at precisely the moment when critical evaluation is most necessary.

The Wharton study provides the clearest evidence that this dependency is not inevitable. Bastani and colleagues provided students with AI tutoring under two conditions: standard access, and a safeguarded mode where the AI used Socratic questioning to guide understanding rather than providing direct answers. When AI access was removed, the standard-access group scored 17% worse than the control. The safeguarded group scored 127% better than the control group that never used AI at all. [5]

The difference between AI that degrades performance and AI that dramatically improves it was not the model, the interface, or the underlying technology. It was whether the interaction was structured to build understanding or to circumvent it. Decomposition — the practice of breaking work into directed and evaluable steps and interrogating outputs against domain knowledge — is the safeguard. Undifferentiated delegation is the trap.

---

## 6. What Decomposition Looks Like in Practice

The term "decomposition" benefits from specificity. Across the studies reviewed here, the highest-performing practitioners shared three concrete behaviors.

First, they **identified sub-steps before engaging AI.** Rather than submitting "build me a function that does X," they separated the problem into component operations: data structure decisions, implementation approach, error handling. Each sub-step was scoped narrowly enough that the practitioner could evaluate the AI's response against their own understanding of the domain.

Second, they **directed the AI through each step** rather than accepting complete outputs. The distinction is between "write me the analysis" and "I'm considering approach X for this constraint set; what are the tradeoffs relative to approach Y?" The first delegates judgment. The second uses AI to inform judgment while retaining it.

Third, they **asked at least one follow-up question per AI output.** Not "is this correct?" — a circular question, asking the system that generated the answer to evaluate it — but rather: "Why this approach instead of the alternative?" or "Under what conditions would this fail?" Questions that require the AI to surface its reasoning, which requires the practitioner to evaluate that reasoning against what they know about the specific context.

This pattern — identify, direct, interrogate — is the behavioral signature of the highest-performing AI users across every study in this analysis. BCG's top-performing practitioners on judgment tasks exhibited precisely this behavior, moving fluidly between AI-generated components and human evaluation without delegating a complete judgment call. [2] The customer service agents who improved most used AI suggestions as inputs to personalized responses, not as finished outputs. [4]

The finding that warrants attention is how uncommon this behavior is. In the Shen study, 14% of developers naturally adopted the high-scoring patterns. [1] The remaining 86% defaulted to some form of delegation — not because they lacked competence, but because delegation requires less effort and produces a subjective experience of productivity that the METR data shows is disconnected from actual performance. [3]

## 7. The Organizational Implication

<Figure5SpendingInversion client:load />

Enterprises currently allocate approximately 93% of AI budgets to technology and 7% to people. [10] BCG's Henderson Institute recommends nearly the inverse: 10% to technology, 20% to process redesign, and 70% to people and change management. [9]

This allocation reflects an assumption that the constraint on AI value is the tool. The evidence reviewed here suggests the binding constraint is the human variable: whether individuals can decompose their work into components where AI delegation is effective and components where human judgment is required, and whether they maintain that distinction under the pressure of daily workflow.

Approximately 6% of organizations qualify as AI high performers by McKinsey's criteria. [8] Those organizations are three times more likely to have redesigned individual workflows at the task level. The pattern is consistent: organizations that capture value from AI are not distinguished by their technology choices but by their investment in the human capability that makes the technology productive.

Deploying AI without developing decomposition capability produces a predictable result: real but modest productivity gains on structured tasks, offset by degraded performance on judgment tasks where the organization's highest-value decisions are concentrated. The 93/7 spending ratio effectively invests in the tool while neglecting the variable that determines whether the tool helps or harms.

---

## 8. The Four-Minute Investment

Sixteen studies, conducted independently across software development, management consulting, customer service, medical practice, and education, converge on the same finding.

AI produces dramatically different outcomes depending on a variable that most organizations are not measuring, most managers cannot observe, and most practitioners are not conscious of. That variable is the mode of collaboration: whether the practitioner decomposes work into directed, evaluable components or delegates whole tasks and accepts outputs without interrogation.

The cost of decomposition is small. Approximately four minutes per task. Two follow-up questions per AI interaction. The returns are large: 62 percentage points in the Shen study [1], the difference between +40% quality and −19% quality in the BCG study [2], the difference between 127% improvement and 17% degradation in the Wharton study. [5]

The Wharton finding is particularly significant because it establishes that decomposition is not a fixed trait. It is a learnable, practicable discipline that produces better outcomes than either unstructured AI use or working without AI entirely. The developers who scored 86% were not more capable than the developers who scored 24%. They had a different practice.

The most underleveraged investment in AI adoption is not a better model, a new platform, or a larger technology budget. It is a discipline: identify the components of a task before submitting it to AI, direct the AI through each component, and ask one follow-up question per output. Four minutes. That is the hidden variable, and the evidence for its impact is substantial.

### Key Studies

**[1]** Shen & Tamkin (Anthropic, 2026) — Analyzed screen recordings of 52 developers using the same AI tool on the same coding task. Identified six distinct interaction patterns; outcomes ranged from 24% to 86%. *(N=52)*

**[2]** Dell'Acqua et al. (Harvard Business School / BCG, 2023) — Measured AI-assisted performance of BCG consultants on structured tasks (+40% quality) versus judgment tasks (−19% vs. control). Introduced the "jagged frontier" framework. *(N=758)*

**[3]** Becker et al. / METR (2025) — Found that developers working on their own projects were 19% slower with AI assistance, while predicting they'd be 24% faster. *(N=16)*

**[4]** Brynjolfsson et al. (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2025) — Studied an AI chat tool's impact on customer service agents at a Fortune 500 company. Found 14% more issues resolved per hour, with largest gains among newer workers. *(N=5,172)*

**[5]** Bastani et al. (Wharton, 2024) — Compared standard AI tutoring vs. Socratic-guided AI tutoring. Standard access led to 17% worse outcomes after AI removal; Socratic access led to 127% improvement vs. control.

**[6]** Budzyń et al. (Lancet Digital Health, 2025) — Endoscopists using an AI detection tool for three months lost 22% of their pre-AI detection accuracy once the tool was removed.

**[7]** Wu et al. (Scientific Reports, 2025) — Four experiments showing AI collaboration reduces intrinsic motivation for independent work, even when participants felt more in control. *(N=3,562)*

**[8]** McKinsey State of AI (2025) — Annual survey of AI adoption and performance across organizations. Approximately 6% qualify as high performers.

**[9]** BCG Henderson Institute — Recommends AI investment allocation of 10% technology / 20% process redesign / 70% people and change management.

**[10]** Deloitte CTO Survey (Fortune, January 2026) — Enterprise AI spending currently runs approximately 93% technology, 7% people.

**[11]** Peng et al. (GitHub Research, 2023) — Randomized controlled trial finding developers using GitHub Copilot completed coding tasks 56% faster. *(N=95)*
